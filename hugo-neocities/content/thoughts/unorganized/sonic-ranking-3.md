So after the last post I thought I'd try and tackle some of the Game Gear/Master System games, just to quickly {{< sup "down" 1 >}} get them out of the way. For this post, I ran through the six mainline 8-bit platformers, which actually ended up being better than I expected overall, though not by a ton.

---

## Sonic the Hedgehog (8-bit)

The first 8-bit outing is... fine. It starts out pretty solid with levels that passingly mimic the feel of the 16-bit originals, albiet scaled way, way down. This game also establishes the trend of throwing traditional Sonic conventions entirely out the window. For starters, the Chaos Emerals aren't gathered from special stages, but are instead hidden in the second acts of each zone. (There is a special stage as well, but it seems to only be for lives/continues/points.) Like Genesis Sonic 1 this game has three acts per zone, but the third act is always a short leadup to the boss without any rings, which IMO helps avoid the bloated feeling of having three full acts to run through.

## Sonic the Hedgehog (8-bit)

On the surface this game is more of the same, but it has a lot of issues that only become apparent after the first zone. To its credit, though, they finally managed to avoid the trope of the Green Hill analogue being the first stage, but only by virtue of Green Hills Zone being the fourth zone instead (why Hills is plural suddenly is anyone's guess). The main hook of this game is that each stage introduces its own unique gimmick, including such highlights as a paraglider with needlessly confusing inputs, bubbles which slowly lift you up underwater segments until you inevitably hit a wall 75% of the way down and are forced to repeat the whole ascent again, and pipe mazes where you move way too fast to have a chance at telling where you're going next.

As far as I can tell, this game entirely lacks special stages. Like before the emeralds are simply hidden in the act 2s, although this time there's an actual reward for collecting them, in the form of a new zone only accessible with all the emeralds. Ultimately the secret zone isn't much to write home about, aside from the bizarrely designed final boss where the winning strategy seems to be hitting the boss once, then immedately ducking into the pipes surrounding the arena, and waiting until Robotnik gets bored of firing every projectile ever made into the center of the arena.

## Sonic Chaos

I actually really enjoyed this one! It's a much smoother experience than the first two (in some ways, maybe a little *too* easy, though) and, most importantly, **you can finally spindash again**. In typical Sonic fashion, the spindash is insanely powerful and lets you skip over a whole ton of stuff if you know what you're doing. The emerald system in this one is very weird: if at any point you collect 100 rings, you're immediately pulled out of the current level and dropped into a special stage {{< sup "down" 2 >}}. There are five special stages, each with their own unique challenges. Also, regardless of whether you get the emerald or not you skip the stage you were on before because there's no law in 8-bit land.

## Sonic Triple Trouble

Triple Trouble is intended as more or less a direct sequel to Chaos, and so it follows a fairly similar structure, although once again the emerald system is entirely unique. In this game, most levels have a hidden emerald monitor, which spawn a warp ring when opened, if and only if the player has at least 50 rings. If not, the monitor is entirely wasted. Unfortuately, the special stages are a lot more frustrating this time. The game alternates between two styles of special stage, a platforming challenge and a ring collecting challenge. The platforming gets brutally punishing, especially the final special stage which requires very specific and careful handling of breakable blocks using movement tech the game never adequately prepares you to use. The ring collection special stages are pretty rough too; you're controlling the Tornado to pick up the rings, but they appear so suddenly that it's impossible to predict where you should move, and your sprite is so big that more often than not it entirely obscures the rings.

The level design overall is a fair bit more frustrating, too. Most of the game is fine, but the final two zones take all the wrong lessons from the earlier games with overly long underwater segments and deliberately obtuse pipe mazes (though the game is merciful enough to give you time to plan out your moves this time).

This game is also noteworthy for being the first appearance of Nack, who as we all know is the one and only Sonic character to wield a gun. What a wacky concept that will never happen again!

Maybe I should check out that 16-bit remake one of these days. I've been meaning to get some fangames on here at some point.

## Sonic Blast

... And then there's Sonic Blast. Hoo boy, Sonic Blast.

So, I (perhaps foolishly) played the Master System port assuming it'd be more tolerable. Granted, being able to see more than an inch in front of you is nice, but this port was so clearly a rush job that I can't help but wonder if it's an overall worse way to play the game.

As you might've guessed from the box art reusing assets from Sonic 3D Blast, the idea here was to design the game using pre-rendered 3D assets like Donkey Kong Country (another platformer series I have, uh, opinions about), albeit to much worse effect. This game is just rough to look at, especially coming off the heel of the much better games that came before.

Once again the rules for special stages are entirely different. Now, like in Sonic 3, special stages are reached by finding giant rings hidden throughout the levels. The special stages themselves are pretty bland, just run into the distance and collect rings as they spawn in. For reasons I cannot even begin to guess, though, only runs on act 2 count for the emeralds. ***NO LAW!***

Most of the levels are pretty whatever, with the notable exception of Blue Marine Zone, which I can only assume was some disgruntled level designer's final revenge against an uncaring world. It's an underwater stage naturally, with the most painfully slow input I've seen in a long time where it genuinely feels like they just entirely disable Sonic's physics every other frame. Just trying to jump {{< sup "down" 1 >}} is so harrowing, especially if there's any other object onscreen. On top of that you have to navigate a pipe maze while also avoiding invisible water currents that will drag you all the way back to the start of the maze if you so much as look in their general direction for too long.

The icing on the cake is the boss, which is fully underwater with no naturally occurring bubbles. If you beat the boss while your timer is too low, you just die. *Techinically* there's a way to manipulate the boss into spawning air bubbles consistently, but it's so unintuitive you'd be very lucky to discover it organically.

Screw it, I'm adding an E rank.
